What the pairs show. Pick either side of a pair and the crowd obeys — gain-frame vs loss-frame, low anchor vs high, opt-in vs opt-out — yet every individual ball stays free and unaware. That is the whole machinery of nudging: we never learn how one person will fall, and we never need to. We move the tribe.

The trap underneath. Leave the fitted bell switched on. It lands smoothly over a skewed crowd, a split crowd, an over-tight crowd — and still reports a tidy mean and standard deviation. If you walked in expecting a bell curve and fed it your data, it hands you a bell curve back. That is not a measurement; it is your assumption wearing the data as a costume. You can read the output forever and never see the two things that actually shaped it: the steering you didn't observe (data bias), and the bell you assumed before you looked (model bias). With an AI model both are hidden at once — the training data carries an entry-angle nobody logged, and the architecture carries pegs nobody chose on purpose — and the fluent, confident, perfectly-shaped output reveals neither.
